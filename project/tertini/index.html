—Partnership™

Tertini produce some of Australia’s finest cool climate, high-altitude wines, however the brand was not communicating a point of difference to the consumer. Instead, it was relying on a generic statement of ‘cool climate wines’. We needed to strengthen the brand’s market positioning and re-design the packaging to set it up for export success.

We identified, that all of their wines are sourced from vineyards growing above 1,600 feet (The lower limit for high altitude classification in Australia) and all within the great dividing mountain range. This high altitude story, combined with the welcoming cellar door approach, we developed the positioning “Over and Above”. Values by which the brand can live by in both specialising in high altitude wines and level of service for customers and cellar door visitors.

The brand identity and label design takes its inspiration from high altitude symbology on maps; the triangle with inner circle is called a ‘trig point’ which identifies a high point. We used this symbology to create an identity with meaning and with relevance in its execution to fit firmly in the premium wine world. The execution features the cool gold T (for Tertini) intertwined with the triangular vine illustration telling the story of the vineyards growing at high points.

Premium print execution is essential on minimalistic label designs. We used an extra thick Manter Cotone Bianco label stock for its embossing qualities, enabling us to achieve a ‘rooftop emboss’ to continue the elevation story with every detail.

The back label is considered just as important as the front label. We continue the story by highlighting the altitude of the vineyard with the trig point map symbol. We feature “OVER AND ABOVE” in a clear gloss high build varnish to remind the consumer what makes this wine special.

Finally the capsule brings visual story telling of the “Great Dividing Range” as a hazy mountainous scape as far as the eye can see – a vista that’s iconic with the high altitude regions of Tertini wines.
